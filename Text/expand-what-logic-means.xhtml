Expansion of what the term "logic" means

Algorithmic Rationalism also expands the idea of what logic is. Conventionally, logic is understood to be a study of how to go from a specific set of premises to a specific set of conclusions. No responsibility is taken for the accuracy of the conclusions of logic aside from them being accurate in the event (however hypothetical) that the premises are true.

Algorithmic Rationalism, however, refers to this area of study as "Hypothetical Logic" - and though we value it as a crucial part of the study of logic, we see the need to expand the understanding of what "logic" means to encompass also a separate area known as "Premise Theory". Premise Theory is the study of how to go about properly acquiring premises for use in logic as well as discerning what level of confidence to place in the premises thus derived. Granted - even Premise Theory can not guarantee the accuracy of the premises that one obtains or of the conclusions that are drawn from said premises. However, if Premise Theory is properly developed and then properly applied, it will nonetheless increase the level of reliability of the premises, even if not to the level of absolute certainty.

Such an expansion of what is understood by he term "logic" is no doubt bound to be controversial. Critics of such an expansion might contend that even if the study of Premise Theory must be undertaken, it shoudln't be regarded as part of the study of logic itself.

However, as controversial as it may be to expand the understanding of what "logic" means in such manner, this position is nonetheless defensible. Part of the reason for this expansion is because it is at long last catching-up the understanding of what "logic" means from one that is congruent with so-called "Continental Rationalism", the old style of Rationalism that eschews empiricism, with the more modern style of rationalism known as "English Rationalism" (despite not being at all exclusive to England or even the English-speaking world) which incorporates empiricism into rationalism. However, this congruity update is not the only reason for expanding this understanding of what "logic" means. It is also necessary because without the study of Premise Theory, the sound development of even Hypothetical Logic beyond the realm of its purely symbolic form can not be undertaken in a sound and useful manner.

Many scholars divide fallacies into two categories - formal and informal. Formal fallacies are flaws in an argument's structure that are reducable to a symbolic form. Informal fallacies, however, are flawed patterns of reasoning that generally aren't reducable to a form that can be expressed in the language of symbolic logic. When you are studying symbolic logic, the formal fallacies are the only fallacies that you have any need to be weary of. However, the moment you expand your study of logic beyond the purely symbolic realm, there immediately appears the need to be vigilant against informal fallacies in even the simplest of arguments.

And unlike with formal fallacies, it is very often impossible to distinguish an informal fallacy from a closely related cogency without resorting to the application of Premise Theory. For example, how else can you distinguish an instance of the False Dilemma Fallacy from an instance from the True Dilemma Cogency? How else can an instance of the Appeal to Consequences Fallacy be distinguished from a cogent form of inverse proof?

In theory one could suggest resolving such an issue by adding every ontological claim on which the cogency of an argument pattern hinges to the list of an argument's conditionals. However, in reality, if this were truly done with consistency, this would result in a practice so unwieldy that at best even a highly intelligent person would have difficulty practicing it in an ivory-tower setting - let alone a far cry from the goal of Algorithmic Rationalsim, which is a system that even ordinary people can learn to practice in everyday settings.

One might resolve this unwieldiness with the short cut of accepting ontological claims that the cogency of certain parts of the argument hinge on without adding them to the list of the argument's conditionals if said ontological claims are sufficiently obvious. This shotrt cut can seem irresistible considering how overwhelming a list of conditionals can become for even a relatively simple argument. However, such an acceptance of even the most obvious ontological claims without a methodology of doing so (i.e. Premise Theory) is a very self-deceptive practice that severely compromises the very integrity of one's study of logic.

It is much better to accept that any study of logic that ventures beyond the realm of mere symbolic logic must necessarily incorporate the study of Premise Theory.
