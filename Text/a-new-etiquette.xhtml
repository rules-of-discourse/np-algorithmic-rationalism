A new standard of etiquette

Part of the goal of Algorithmic Rationalism is to introduce a new standard of etiquette to supplement pre-existing ones wherever the two are compatible and to supplant pre-existing ones on whatever matters they are at irreconcilable conflict.

There are plenty of codes of etiquette that exist int he world. Each one is generally written to serve various purposes, but rarely if ever is assuring the primacy of logic, reason, and evidence among those purposes. When it is, it tends to at times be overshadowed by other goals getting prioritized above it. Even codes of etiquette written expressly for the purpose of debate generally tend to strongly oppose rude or disorderly conduct, but have few if any portections against far more pernicious vices such as the use of deceptive rhetoric.

Granted, sometimes it can be difficult to include protections against such things in the debate's code of etiquette, as doing so might deter parties who wish to indulge in said unsavory behavior from participating in the debate. However, if the target audience of the debate were to strongly hold to a philosophy such as Algorithmic Rationalism, that might shift the scales of consideration in favor of including such provisions in the rules of the debate - as the credibility of the debate might rely on it. Similarly, it may in some cases even pressure a party that would otherwise be unwilling to participate in a debate in which logic, reason, and evidence have to actually be respected into doing so - or at least force them to openly expose unwillingness to try their views in the court of rationality.

In addition to merely asserting the importance of our codes of etiquette making logic, reason, and evidence a higher priority, part of the effort in developing Algorithmic Rationalism is the Rules of Discourse Project, whose sole publication currently is the Discursive Compendium of Fallacies and Malconducts. This document (currently an early work-in-progress, yet freely available as a Free Source project) strives to provide clear criteria for discerning whether or not a particular fallacy has been used, but also strives to describe what is and isn't proper conduct for debate, or any discussion, formal or informal, in a society that values rationality. It, too, does not intend to supplant pre-existing codes of etiquette - yet it does condemn invoking or enforcing those pre-existing codes in a manner that is at the expense of the unhindered pursuit of truth.
