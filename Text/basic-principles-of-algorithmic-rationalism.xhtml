Basic Principles of Algorithmic Rationalism

Only one thing is sacrosanct in Algorithmic Rationalism, and that is the conviction that people ought to follow the dictates of logic, reason, and evidence wherever they lead. Consequently, there is only one thing that is taboo in Algorithmic Rationalism, and that taboo is to go against the one sacrosanct.

As for the methodologies used in Algorithmic Rationalism, the imperative to follow them is not a separate thing from the one sacrosanct, but an extension of it. This is because you are not truly following the dictates of logic, reason, and evidence wherever they lead if you violate the process by which these dictates are followed.

Very prominent among these methodologies is the one from which Algorithmic Rationalism derives its name - that being the use of razor algorithms to resolve matters of uncertainty in logic and reason. When it is uncertain whether or not a particular fallacy or malconduct has been committed, an algorithm can be devised whose outcome would indicate the answer to the uncertainty. These algorithms could range anywhere from a simple razor question to an elaborate tree of several razor questions.

Some such algorithms are contained in the Discursive Compendium of Fallacies and Malconducts - and while the algorithms there-contained are by no means guaranteed to be perfect, they do serve as a good example of how such algorithms ought to be drawn up.

While many people have come to think of algorithms as part of the world of computers (and often think of them as the nefarious constructs that internet media companies use to manipulate people) algorithms are not necessarily nefarious, nor are they necessarily tied to computers. These algorithms are not designed to be followed by computers at all, but by human beings who wish to come to as honest an understanding as possible with regards to the solidity of reason and discourse.
